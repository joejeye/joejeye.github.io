Daytime Hours

Posted on 2023-09-25

Acknowledgement

The post utilizes Julia programming language to perform computations and to generate figures.

Introduction

It is well-known that the daytime length varies with the latitude and the day in a year. The definition of daytime from Wikipedia is[1]

Daytime as observed on Earth is the period of the day during which a given location experiences natural illumination from direct sunlight.

Apart from direct meaurement, the daytime length can also be acquired by computation (with some ideal assumptions though). There are some formulae for computing the daytime length, involving atronomical notions such as hour angle[2] and sun declination[3], and even some websites, such as SOLAR TOPO[4] and Daylight Hours Explorer[5], providing the functionality that user can interactively query the daytime length at certain latitude and certain day in a year. There is also a formulae derivation[6], which relies on some empirical constants.

In this post, I describe my way of deriving the algorithm computing the daytime length (expressed in hours).

Assumptions

To make our lives easier in deriving the algorithm, we make some assumptions first.

Assumptions:

  1. Earth's orbit around Sun is a perfect circle, with Sun being the circle center.
  2. The shape of Earth is a perfect sphere.
  3. Sun light rays received by Earth are all parallel to the line segment connecting Sun and Earth.
  4. Do not account for atomspheric refraction.
  5. 1 year is precisely 365 days.
  6. 1 day is precisely 24 hours.
  7. Winter solstice is on December 21st, and summer solstice is on June 21st.

Coordinate Systems and Coordinate Transformations

It is known that Earth has an axial tilt of about 23.44 degrees. This tilt is the cause of daytime lenght variantion throughout a year. Figure B illustrates the factors that affect daytime length.

where the parallel black circles on the sphere are the curves of latitude, the yellow circle is the illumination circle, the blue straight line is Earth's rotational axis, and the coordinate system is selected such that the origin is located at Earth center, the z-axis is parallel to Earth's orbital axis and pointing towards North, and the x-axis and y-axis are determined by right-hand rule.

As seen in Figure B, the daytime length for a certain latitude at a certain time of a year is the portion of the latitude curve that lies in the day-side of the illumination circle. With the chosen coordinate system in Figure B, time progressing through a year is equivalent to the yellow circle rotating about the z-axis. A straightforward way to compute daytime length is to find where the illumination circle and the latitude circle of interest intersect. To accomplish this, we need to descibe the circles using equations, which might be a bit involved if we were to base our computation solely on the coordinate system in Figure B. Some other coordinate systems for certain circles can make the equations more decent. When using different coordinate systems for different curves, we need to employ coordiate transformations in order to find intersections.

Coordinate Transformations

So far we have equations for latitude circles and the illumination circle, the next thing to do is to put those equations together to find intersections, and thus we will need the coordinate transformation between ACS and ICS. The OCS we have established plays the role of a bridge connecting ACS and ICS – It is trivial to describe the coordinate transformations between OCS and ACS, as well as that between OCS and ICS, because they are rotation matrices whose rotational axes are OCS coordinate axes.

Find The Intersections

Recall that finding the daytime length is equivalent to finding the intersections of the illumination circle and the latitude circle, and the circles are defined by equations under their respective coordinate systems.
